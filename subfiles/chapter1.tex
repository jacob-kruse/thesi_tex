\chapter{Introduction}
Reinforcement Learning (RL) has achieved remarkable success in simulation, where agents can execute many actions and easily evaluate rewards. For instance, \cite{SimGrasp} trains a robotic manipulator in simulation to perform a variety of manipulation tasks, while \cite{DeepQ} applies RL to teach an agent to play Atarti games. Recently, RL has been increasingly applied to real-world tasks, motivated by the desire to tackle challenging problems beyond simulation. For example, \cite{LegRL} demonstrates how a legged robot can learn to traverse real world terrains, and \cite{wu2023daydreamer} shows the application of their method to multiple physical platforms, including a quadruped robot, a robotic manipulator, and Sphero robot. Another prominent application of real-world RL is robotic grasping \cite{ManipSurvey}, due to its widespread relevance in manipulation tasks. However, practical deployment of RL in the real world faces several challenges. Uncertainty in sensing and actuation, high-dimensional continuous state and action spaces, the high cost of collecting large amounts of data, and the need to ensure safety during exploration all complicate the learning process.
% These challenges motivate the development of methods that can learn effective policies from limited, noisy data while robustly executing learned tasks in real-world environments.

Offline RL is a popular strategy for physical RL because it allows agents to learn from pre-colelcted datasets. This greatly improves the sample efficiency by enabling the algorithm to focus entirely on policy optimization rather than exploration. To address the main challenge of distribution shift in offline RL, techniques such as regularization and policy constraints are commonly applied. Regularization involves adding a term to the learning objective that encourages desirable behaviors in the learned policy, such as safer actions, improved exploration, or policy outputs that stay close to the data distribution. Similarly, Maximum Entropy Reinforcement Learning (MaxEnt RL) \cite{MaxEntRL} alters the standard RL objective by adding an entropy term to the reward. This encourages the policy to maximize both expected return and entropy, promting stochasticity in the policy,
% The entropy term incentivizes broader action distributions, which improves exploration and can enhance robustness
Although this resembles regularization with the additional term, MaxEnt RL fundamentally redefines the optimization objective. A notable example is Soft Actor-Critic (SAC) \cite{SAC}, which optimizes the MaxEnt objective. The method proposed in this work takes a similar approach by redefining the objective to include an additional term aimed at increasing action robustness.

\section{Contributions}
In this paper, we introduce a framework that learns an approximate reward model and leverages it for iterative policy optimization with a novel robustness-oriented objective. Our approach is inspired by ideas from SAC and MaxEnt RL but adapts them to a contextual bandit setting suitable for grasping tasks. The main contributions of this work are:

\begin{itemize}

    \item A new learning objective, the Maximum Action Noise Threshold (MaxANT), which encourages selection of actions that are both high-reward and robust to perturbations.

    \item Experiments demonstrating the method on a robotic sorting task, showing improved performance and robustness compared to standard policy optimization.

    \item An iterative learning algorithm that refines both the reward model and policy by combining expert, random, and on-policy data to efficiently improve grasping performance.

\end{itemize}

    \item \textbf{Chapter 3 - Preliminaries:} This section details the foundational concepts that pertain to this approach. Section 3.1 defines the Contextual Bandit framework while Section 3.2 provides the basics of learning a reward model with supervised learning.

Section 4.1 explains the strategies employed to learn an accurate reward model with an effective initial data collection strategy and with the use of ensemble networks to capture uncertainty.     \item \textbf{Chapter 5 - Experiments:} This section explains the experiments that were conducted to evaluate the method. A robotic grasp experimental setup and the details are explained here.

    \item \textbf{Chapter 6 - Conclusion:} This final chapter summarizes the work and discusses the implications.

\end{itemize}
