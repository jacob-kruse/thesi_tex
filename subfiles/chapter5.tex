\chapter{Experiments}
\label{Experiments}

To evaluate the proposed method, experiments were done on a sorting task with a robotic manipulator. The following experiments are the main contributions of this paper. 

\section{Task Description}
The proposed task is to autonomously sort recyclable items within a constrained workspace. The item set consists of aluminum cans and plastic bottles with varying degrees of deformation, which introduces variability in shape and grasp difficulty. The manipulation is performed with a KUKA LBR iisy 3 r760 collaborative robot fitted with an Ubiros Gentle Pro gripper at the end effector. The workspace state is captured using an overhead Oak-D S2 RGBD camera and subsequently encoded to form the input to the learned policy, which outputs the corresponding control action for the manipulator. After executing the action, the robot automatically closes the gripper, moves to the recycling bin, and opens the gripper to release any grasped object. An action is considered successful if the assigned object is grasped and transported to the recycling bin. The overall sorting procedure is summarized in Algorithm \ref{Sorting Loop}.

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{Sorting}
\label{Sorting Loop}
\textbf{Input}: Learned policy $\pi_\phi$
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\While {items in workspace}
    \State Camera obtains state $s$ and encodes it as $s_\phi$
    \State Robot moves to ``Home" position
    \State Get policy action  $a \gets \pi_\phi(s_\phi)$
    \State Robot executes the policy action 
    \State Robot closes gripper to grab item
    \State Robot moves to ``Bin" position
    \State Robot opens gripper to drop item
\EndWhile
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Context/State Estimation}
The workspace state is obtained from an overhead RGBD camera. When the current state is requested, the camera captures an RGB and a corresponding depth image of the workspace. The RGB image is passed though a YOLO v11 instance segmentation model to acquire pixle-wise masks of bottles and cans present in the scene. The YOLO model was trained on a combination of publicly available datasets containing annotated images of general cans and bottles, along with custom images collected from the workspace with manual annotation. After obtaining the segmented instances, Principal Component Analysis (PCA) \cite{21} is performed on each mask to compute the centroid and principal axis of the objects. 
%
% PCA provides the dominant direction of variance in the mask, which corresponds to the object's primary geometric axis in the image plane.
%
The corresponding depth image is then used to recover the 3D position of the centroid. Using camera intrinsics, ray projection, and ROS2 coordinate frame transformations, the centroid is transformed into the robot's frame. The principal axis extracted from PCA defines the object's yaw angle in the robot frame. To estimate the pitch angle, a two-point method is employed. Two points located near opposite ends of the object along the principal axis are strategically chosen. Like the centroid, the 3D position of these points are computed in the robot frame using the depth image. Let the vertical change between the two points be $\Delta z$, and let the planar displacement be in the $xy$-plane be $\Delta xy$. The pitch angle is then computed by $\vartheta=\arctan{\frac{\Delta z}{\Delta xy}}$. Due to the axial symmetry of bottles and cans, the roll angle is assumed to be zero. Therefore, the full 6D pose of each object is defined by the 3D position of the centroid and the estimated pitch and yaw angles.

\section{Context/State Definition}
Because the number of items in the workspace varies and the robot grasps one item at a time, only the 6D pose of a single target is considered in the state representation. The state captures the 3D position and orientation of the current object selected for grasping, along with contextual information about the workspace. The state is defined as: 

\[
s=[x, y, z, \vartheta, \psi, t,n]
\]

% \[
% x=[\text{x} , \text{y}, \text{z}, \vartheta, \psi, t,n]
% \]

where $x,y,z \in \mathbb{R}$ represent the Eucledian coordinates of the object's centroid, 
% where x, y, z $\in \mathbb{R}$ represent the eucledian coordinates of the object's centroid,
$\vartheta$ and $\psi$ denote the pitch and yaw angles desribing the object's orientation, $t$ represents the object type (e.g., can or bottle), and $n$ denotes the number of objects currently present in the scene. Thus, the state respresents the 6D pose of the active object and basic scene-level information. Due to the physical constraints of the workspace, the following bounds are imposed on the state variables: 

\[
\begin{gathered}
x \in [0.20, 0.55], \
y \in [-0.35, 0.35], \
z \in [0.80, 0.95],  \\
\vartheta \in [-\frac{\pi}{2}, 0], \
\psi \in [-\pi, \pi]
\end{gathered}
\]

Because the true state space is continuous, there are infinitely many possible states. To significantly reduce the size of the state space for the reward and policy networks, a discretized encoding is introduced. First, the workspace is divided into two equal partitions along the $x$ and $y$ axes, producing four spatial quadrants. Then, the pitch angle $\vartheta$ range is divided into three uniform bins, and the yaw angle $\psi$ range is divided into four uniform bins. The object type $t$ is preserved, while the number of objects $n$ is removed from the discretized representation. The resulting encoded state is defined as:

\[
\hat s = [q,w,v,t],
\]

% \[
% \hat x = [q,w,s,t],
% \]

where $q \in \{ 1,2,3,4 \}$ denotes the spatial quadrant of the object, $w \in \{ 1,2,3 \}$ represents the discretized pitch bin, $v \in \{ 1,2,3,4 \}$ denotes the discretized yaw bin, and $t$ represents the object type. This encoding reduces the continuous state to a compact representation suitable for reward modeling and policy learning.

\section{Action Definition}
The actions for the sorting task define how the robotic manipulator moves prior to attempting a grasp. Each action specifies a desired relative displacement of the tool center point (TCP), i.e., the center of the gripper, with respect to the current object pose. To execute an action, the robot computes an inverse kinematics \cite{22} solution and plans a motion to the specified pose. The action is 6-dimensional, continuous, and is defined by the following 

\[
a = [\Delta l, \Delta w, \Delta h, \Delta \varphi, \Delta \vartheta, \Delta \psi]
\]

where $\Delta l, \Delta w, \Delta h$ are the relative Cartesian offsets in meters along the object's local XYZ frame, and $\Delta \varphi, \Delta \vartheta, \Delta \psi$ represent the relative orientation offsets (roll, pitch, and yaw, respectively) in radians. All position and orientation components are defined relative to the pose of the target object. This means that the action does not command an absolute pose in the workspace frame, but rather a pose expressed in the object's local frame. For example, the action $[0.0, 0.0, 0.0, \frac{\pi}{4}, 0.0, 0.0]$ moves the TCP to the object's centroid with matching pitch and yaw, while introducing a relative roll offset of $45^\circ$. Each component of the relative action is clamped to pre-defined bounds defined by

\[
\begin{gathered}
\Delta l \in [-0.10, 0.10], \
\Delta w \in [-0.10, 0.10], \
\Delta h \in [-0.01, 0.10],  \\
\Delta \varphi \in [-\frac{\pi}{4}, \frac{\pi}{4}], \
\Delta \vartheta \in [-\frac{\pi}{4}, \frac{\pi}{4}], \
\Delta \psi \in [-\frac{\pi}{4}, \frac{\pi}{4}]
\end{gathered}
\]

to ensure the manipulator only attempts feasible grasps.

Although the policy outputs a relative action, execution requires an absolute pose in the workspace frame. The absolute action is computed with component-wise addition between the action and the true state defined in the previous section. Before execution, the resulting absolute target pose is clamped to the same bounds defined for the workspace state.

\section{Reward Definition}
Rewards are assigned autonomously to each action executed in the workspace. The reward is binary: actions that result in a successful grasp receive a reward of 1, while unsuccessful actions receive a reward of 0. Action success is determined using two criteria: a reduction in the total workspace items, and the measured current load of the gripper. If the number of items in the workspace decreases by one following the action and gripper current exceeds 150 mA, the action is classified as successful and assigned a reward of 1. Otherwise, the action is classified as a failure and assigned a reward of 0. This dual-condition check ensures that grasp attempts that fail to grab the object are not incorrectly labeled as successful.

To train the reward model, Binary Cross-Entropy (BCE) loss is used. Cross-Entropy loss \cite{20} is widely used for probability models and classification tasks. Because the reward data takes on one of two labels (0 or 1), the binary variant of Cross-Entropy is appropriate. The Binary Cross-Entropy is defined as

\[
\mathcal{L}_{BCE}(\theta) =
-\frac{1}{B}\sum_{b=1}^B
y_b\cdot \log(\hat y_b) + 
(1-y_b) \cdot \log(1-\hat y_b)
\]

where $y$ denotes the true labels of input $x$ and $\hat y$ represents the predicted labels  produced by a function $f_\theta(x)$. Minimizing this loss encourages the reward model to assign high probability to successful actions and low probability to unsuccessful ones. 

A binary reward setup is effective for evaluating the method because multiple actions can yield successful grasps and achieve the maximum reward. However, many of these actions are suboptimal due to inherent uncertainty in the workspace and the grasping process. Rather than optimizing for any action that simply achieves success, the modified objective encourages convergence towards actions that are more optimal and robust to uncertainty. These optimal actions are often located near the center of regions with consistently high rewards. Converging to these regions improves overall performance and helps avoid policies that settle in local optima or rely on highly situational actions.

\section{Initial Dataset}
As mentioned earlier, the goal of the initial data collection phase is to produce a dataset with diverse states, actions, and rewards. To achieve this, data was collected from both an expert and a random policy to ensure a mix of successful and unsuccessful interactions. Expert data was generated by hand-guiding the KUKA collaborative robot to a variety of feasible grasp poses for the assigned object. With this teaching process, 160 expert demonstrations were produced, corresponding to five demonstrations per state. Additionally, a random policy was implemented, sampling actions uniformly within the action bounds. Executing this policy yielded 340 additional interactions with mainly unsuccessful attempts to provide negative reward examples. Together, the expert and random data form an initial dataset of 500 interactions, containing both high quality grasps and exploration failures to produce an effective initial reward model.
